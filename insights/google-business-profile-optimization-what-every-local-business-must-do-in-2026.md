1. Fix Your Primary Category First

This is one of the biggest ranking levers you have, and a lot of businesses get it wrong right out of the gate.

Your primary category tells Google what your business mainly does.

If that’s wrong, fuzzy, or too broad, everything gets harder.

Common mistake:

A business picks whatever sounds the most “professional” instead of what actually matches what they sell most.

Bad examples:
“Contractor”
“Consultant”
“Service”
“Home Improvement”

That tells Google almost nothing.

Better examples:
“Plumber”
“HVAC contractor”
“Landscape lighting designer”
“Electrician”
“Roofing contractor”
“Medical spa”

Be specific.

Not clever.
Not broad.
Specific.

Action step:

Open Google Maps and look at the top 3 businesses outranking you for your main service.

Check:

their primary category
their secondary categories
whether yours actually lines up

If you are categorized wrong, fix that first.

That one move alone can make a difference.

2. Stop Stuffing Keywords Into Your Business Name

This one needs to die already.

If your business is called:

“Smith Plumbing”

…do not turn it into:

“Smith Plumbing Water Heater Drain Cleaning Emergency Plumber Tampa”

That is not smart.
That is desperate.

And yes, some businesses still get away with it.

Until they don’t.

Google wants your business name to reflect your real business name, not your wishlist of keywords.

And if you’re building your visibility on shaky stuff like that, you are building on sand.

What to do instead:

Keep your business name clean and real.

Then optimize the places that are actually supposed to be optimized:

categories
services
reviews
photos
description
website pages
service area setup

That’s where the real wins are.

Not keyword graffiti.

3. Fill Out the Entire Profile — Not Just the Lazy Parts

A half-complete Google Business Profile is one of the easiest ways to lose trust and relevance.

Google is trying to figure out:

What do you do?
Where do you do it?
Can it trust you enough to show you?

If your profile barely answers those questions, why would it rank you?

Here’s what should be fully completed:
Business name
Primary category
Secondary categories
Phone number
Website
Business hours
Holiday hours
Service areas
Business description
Services
Booking / appointment link
Attributes
Photos
Logo
Cover image

If you have skipped half of that, you are making this harder than it needs to be.

Action step:

Open your profile and ask yourself:

“If I knew nothing about my business, would this profile clearly tell me what we do, where we do it, and why I should trust us?”

If the answer is “kind of,” it needs work.

“Kind of” does not rank.

4. If You’re a Service Business, Your Setup Needs to Match Reality

This is a big one.

If customers do not come to your location, your profile should not be pretending they do.

A lot of service businesses mess this up.

They:

use a home address publicly
use a mailbox
use a virtual office
set up fake locations to “rank in more cities”

That might work for a while.

Then one day:

your profile gets flagged
rankings tank
visibility disappears
and now you’re filling out suspension forms instead of booking jobs

That’s a terrible trade.

What to do:

If you go to the customer, your setup should reflect that.

Make sure your:

address setup is clean
service area is accurate
website matches your service footprint
citations match your real-world setup

You do not need tricks.

You need consistency.

That wins longer.

5. Your Description Should Sound Like a Real Business, Not a Brochure

This is where a lot of businesses lose the plot.

They write descriptions like this:

“We pride ourselves on providing exceptional customer service and quality workmanship to all of our valued clients.”

That means absolutely nothing.

Nobody talks like that.
Nobody believes that.
Google learns nothing from that.

Your description should answer 4 things:
What do you do?
Who do you help?
What are you best known for?
Why should someone trust you?

That’s it.

Better example:

We help homeowners across Palm Beach County with outdoor lighting, landscape lighting, and pool enclosure lighting that makes homes safer, cleaner, and better looking at night. From design to install, we focus on quality work, clean installs, and systems built to last.

That works.

It’s clear.
It sounds human.
It says something.

Action step:

Rewrite your description so it sounds like you are explaining your business to a customer in 15 seconds.

If it sounds like corporate oatmeal, rewrite it.

6. Your Services Section Should Actually Reflect What You Sell

This is one of the most underused parts of the profile.

A lot of businesses either:

leave it blank
let Google auto-fill nonsense
or just list broad junk like “Plumbing” or “Roofing”

That is lazy.

And it leaves relevance on the table.

What to do:

List the actual things customers search for.

Example:

If you are a plumber, your services should include things like:

Water heater repair
Drain cleaning
Leak detection
Toilet installation
Sewer line repair
Emergency plumbing
Tankless water heater install

If you are a med spa:

Botox
Lip filler
Microneedling
Chemical peels
Weight loss consultations
Skin tightening

If you are a roofer:

Roof replacement
Roof repair
Storm damage repair
Tile roofing
Shingle roofing
Flat roof repair
Action step:

Go to your website.
Look at your actual money-making services.
Then make sure those are reflected inside your profile.

If your GBP is vague, your rankings usually are too.

8. Reviews Need a Real System — Not Hope

A lot of business owners say they want more reviews.

Then they ask for them:

randomly
inconsistently
only when they remember
and only after “perfect” jobs

That is not a system.

That is wishful thinking.

And meanwhile your competitor is stacking fresh reviews every week and pulling ahead.

What Google and customers both want to see:
strong review volume
fresh review activity
service-specific language
owner responses
real trust
Here’s how to do this properly:

Ask every happy customer.

Not some.
Not “the really nice ones.”
Every happy customer.

Best timing:

Ask right after:

install completion
successful repair
project handoff
happy walkthrough
positive service interaction
Best ask:

Do not say:

“Can you leave us a review?”

Instead say something like:

“If you have 30 seconds, would you mind sharing what work we did for you and how it went? It really helps.”

That tends to get much better reviews.

Why?

Because now they mention:

the service
the experience
the result

That’s useful.

Also:

Reply to every review.

Not with robotic nonsense.

Actually respond like a business owner who gives a damn.

9. Google Posts Still Matter — If You Use Them Like a Smart Business

Most businesses either:

ignore posts
or
post garbage

There’s not much middle ground.

Good GBP posts look like:
before and after projects
seasonal reminders
service spotlights
quick homeowner tips
“what to know before hiring”
recent installs
financing updates
short FAQ-style posts
Bad GBP posts look like:
“Happy Tuesday!”
giant blocks of text
stock images
cheesy holiday filler
obvious AI sludge
low-effort Canva junk with zero context

Nobody is booking because you posted “Happy Labor Day from the team.”

Stop it.

Action step:

Post 1–2 useful updates per week.

Keep them short.
Use real photos.
Say something helpful.
Make it about the customer.

That’s enough.

10. Your Website and Google Business Profile Need to Match

This is where a lot of businesses accidentally confuse Google.

Your profile says one thing.

Your website says another.

Your reviews say something else.

Now Google is trying to figure out:

“What exactly does this business actually do?”

That confusion costs visibility.

Your website should clearly reinforce:
your main services
your service areas
your trust signals
your business identity
your contact details
your reviews
your authority

If your profile says:

“landscape lighting”
“pool enclosure lighting”
“Palm Beach County”

…but your website barely mentions those clearly, you are weakening your own local visibility.

Action step:

Make sure your website has:

clear service pages
clear location relevance
matching contact info
matching business positioning
useful FAQs
strong proof

This is not just a Google Business Profile issue.

This is a clarity issue.

And clarity wins.
